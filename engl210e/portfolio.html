Chapter 3: Writing Collaboratively

This section includes the timesheet template, original timesheet guidelines document, and revised timesheet guidelines document.

Chapter 3 of Practical Strategies for Technical Communication places a large emphasis on setting up collaborative writing environments. From this section, learning how to maintain an accurate schedule and monitor the project were vital for the Orange Draft group’s success. As the facilitator, I created a timesheet template for the team to track the time spent working on the student handbook. By tracking progress with the timesheet, team members could plan ahead and allocate tasks in accordance to the progress of others.

Markel discusses the importance of creating a style sheet that “defines the characteristics of the document’s writing style”. With timesheets being an integral part of the handover packages, serving as a receipt for the hours worked, it was important that they were accurate and of the same quality as all other documents. To enforce a similar writing style, I introduced a Timesheet Guidelines directive document to supplement the timesheets – explaining all timesheet maintenance procedures.

  • Original Timesheet Guidelines

The original version was created prior to covering the topic of directives and thus failed to include the elements required for an effective directive. Specifically, it did not present explanations for why the procedures were desirable or necessary, and failed to convey a modest and cooperative tone. The timesheet guideline directive was eventually rewritten to pose as a more compelling and persuasive document.

Chapter 5: Research Subject

This section includes the student survey and survey rationale documents.

The Orange Draft team decided to administer a survey as the primary method of gathering information from our audience. The main objective of the survey was to collect the information required as simply and efficiently as possible. This was done using a variety of question strategies including scale, multiple choice, and short answer questions. It was important to gather enough useful information whist presenting a document that was short enough to complete in a reasonable amount of time.

I developed the survey to feature a total of 32 multiple choice and 9 short answer questions. The survey gathered data on the 4 main sections that the Orange Draft team was originally considering to include in the student handbook – food, university life, navigation, and the mathematics faculty.

  • The survey features gender inclusive language by posing questions in second person and avoids biased language by ensuring no loaded or leading questions.
  • The survey is designed in such a way that responses can be easily grouped by academic term, with each question’s language adjusted to the current academic term of the correspondent.
  • The survey includes an introductory explanation that clearly indicates who is administering the survey, the intended use of the data, and the deadline to submit a response.

Chapters 7 + 8: Creating Graphics and Designing Documents

This section includes screenshots and analysis of the student handbook website.

The Orange Draft group decided to use a web-based format for the student handbook to make it more easily accessible to students and incorporate augmented functionality. As the main web developer of uwhandbook.online, I was responsible for designing and developing every aspect of the website, excluding the Student Life section.

During development, I closely referenced and followed the 5 goals of document design from Practical Strategies for Technical Communication.

  1. To make a good impression on readers
  2. To help readers understand the structure and hierarchy of the information
  3. To help the readers find the information they need
  4. To help readers understand the information
  5. To help readers remember the information

  • Make a Good Impression

    To make a good impression on the reader, I focused on giving the handbook a professional look and feel that reflects the professional standards of the team. Each page has a header and footer that includes the Orange Draft team logo, adheres to the Orange Draft branding colors, and features a similar design style.

  • Help Readers Understand Structure

    Design principles and graphics are both utilised in the student handbook to help the readers understand the structure and hierarchy of the information. The principle of alignment was used to illustrate relationships and help organize information, whereas the principle of proximity was used to relate two items to one another. In the on-campus food section, alignment is what differentiates a restaurant chain from an individual store. In the Tim Hortons instructions, proximity is what connects the navigation steps to their corresponding images. By boxing and aligning content to the right of screen, proximity and alignment indicate that the information is relevant but not intended to be the main content of the page.

    Notable graphics include the organizational chart in the faculty and courses section, explicitly conveying the hierarchy and relationship of first-year courses. The header of each page features a file path illustration to help users navigate the website and understand the hierarchy of the website’s content.

  • Help Readers Find Information

    To help the readers find the information they need, I took advantage of different design elements such as contrast, color and typography. Good contrast, sans serif typefaces, and large type sizes all make the content easy to read on different screens and in different environments. The orange accent color typically denotes interactable items and bold text typically signifies importance. Various different headings and rules are used to separate sections and subsections. The homepage index table groups information in different categories so that users can find the information they need quicker.

  • Help Readers Understand Information

    Preattentive attributes such as symbols and colors are used throughout the handbook to help readers understand the information. Icon grids in the student services, student life, and food sections help the reader relate and correlate information to their memory. The off-campus food section utilises shades of green and red to identify how good or bad a metric score is. To help users better understand the information, items such as resource links and expandable checklists provide additional information and explanations about the content. Maps present spatial information to the user that is difficult to communicate with words, with legends helping the user interpret them.

  • Help Readers Remember Information

    The principle of repetition is used throughout the handbook to help the reader remember the information. Repeated visuals and patterns such as the icon grid, image splashes, headers, rules and additional information boxes are used to create a sense of memorability for the website and its content. Repeating designs and patterns with external connotations like the paperback design also allows the user to associate the information to past memories and experiences.

Chapter 9: Writing Correspondence

This section includes screenshots and analysis of team correspondence.

With busy schedules often interfering with one another, it was difficult for the Orange Draft group to meet in-person and communicate face-to-face. As a result, the majority of team-wide communication took place through the Slack group – where the team planned deliverables, set deadlines, allocated tasks, and relayed progress updates.

Slack: cloud-based instant-messaging service for team collaboration.

In Slack, workspaces are comprised of multiple channels with different purposes and topics – allowing for different correspondence formalities to coexist in the same communication platform. For example, the #general channel was reserved for general work-related communication and often exhibited moderately formal correspondence. In contrast, the #random channel was reserved for non work-related communication and exhibited informal correspondence.

To present myself effectively in correspondence, I strived to follow the five correspondence suggestions from Practical Strategies for Technical Communication.

  1. Use the appropriate level of formality
  2. Communicate correctly
  3. Project the “you” attitude
  4. Avoid correspondence clichés
  5. Communicate honestly

  • Appropriate Level of Formality

    To use an appropriate level of formality, I ensured that all messages matched the formality of the channel in which they were sent. Messages sent through direct messages or the #random channel typically featured informal language with slang, emojis, incomplete sentences, and abbreviations. Messages sent through the #general channel featured a moderately informal tone using complete and complex sentences with minimal abbreviations. Correct communication was only applicable to moderately formal correspondence, where communication was typically grammar and spelling -mistake free.

  • Project the “You” Attitude

    To project the “you” attitude, I conveyed a courteous and positive tone in all correspondence. I refrained from explicitly blaming others, and ensured that I always included a compliment when critiquing the work of others. I took into consideration and ensured that my correspondence met the interests and needs of all teammates. My communication in the #general channel was to the point, result driven and proactive.

  • Communicate Honestly

    It was important that I communicated honestly with teammates. I ensured to accept and own up to mistakes when they occurred, as well as set reasonable deadlines and reported on my progress accurately.
